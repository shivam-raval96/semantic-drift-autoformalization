You will read a short story and invent your own formal notation to represent
it precisely. No notation is provided — designing one is part of the task.

## What these stories contain

Each story describes a workplace with a repeated action that combines two
things into one. The order of the two things matters: doing the action to
"this and that" is not assumed to give the same result as doing it to "that
and this".

Each story has two parts:

1. A custom that always holds, without exception: whenever the worker picks
   starting things and runs the described procedures, the two named results
   always come out the same.
2. A closing question, asking whether a second regularity must also always
   hold in that workplace.

## Example stories

The stories under this heading are context only: they show the range of
stories your notation must be able to represent. Do not represent them in
your answer.

### Example story 1

In a certain paint workshop, the colorist follows one unbreakable habit. Take any two pigments at all — call the first crimson and the second ochre.

She pours ochre into crimson and sets the result aside as Batch 1; then she pours crimson into Batch 1 and calls the result Batch 2.

However she chooses her two starting pigments, crimson itself and Batch 2 always come out the exact same color. That is simply how this workshop works, without exception.

One morning her apprentice wonders about something. Take any four pigments — again call the first crimson, the second ochre, the third teal, and the fourth indigo. Pour crimson into ochre and set the result aside as Batch 1. Separately, pour indigo into ochre and call the result Batch 2; then pour teal into Batch 2 and call that Batch 3; then pour Batch 3 into crimson and set the result aside as Batch 4.

In this workshop, must Batch 1 and Batch 4 always come out the same color?

### Example story 2

In a certain mountain teahouse, the tea master follows one unbreakable habit. Take any two teas at all — call the first jasmine and the second oolong.

He pours jasmine over the leaves of jasmine and sets the result aside as Brew 1; then he pours oolong over the leaves of Brew 1 and calls the result Brew 2.

However he chooses his two starting teas, jasmine itself and Brew 2 always taste exactly the same. That is simply how this teahouse works, without exception.

One evening his newest server wonders about something. Take any four teas — again call the first jasmine, the second oolong, the third rooibos, and the fourth sencha. Pour oolong over the leaves of rooibos and set the result aside as Brew 1; then pour oolong over the leaves of sencha and call the result Brew 2; then pour oolong over the leaves of Brew 2 and call that Brew 3; then pour Brew 1 over the leaves of Brew 3 and set the result aside as Brew 4.

In this teahouse, must jasmine itself and Brew 4 always taste the same?

## Your task

Invent a rigid, unambiguous formal notation capable of representing any
story of this kind, then represent the target story below in it. You may
choose any symbols and structure you like, as long as:

- **It is fully specified.** After reading your specification alone, someone
  else could write down, or read off, the representation of such a story
  without guessing.
- **It is unambiguous.** Every well-formed statement in your notation has
  exactly one meaning.
- **It is complete but minimal.** It captures which starting things are
  freely chosen, each performance of the combining action together with the
  order of its two participants, which results the custom says always come
  out the same, and what the closing question asks — and nothing else: no
  workers, no settings, no narrative.
- **It separates asserting from asking.** A reader of the representation
  can tell what the story asserts always holds from what it merely asks.

## The target story

At a certain mountaintop relay station, the operator follows one unbreakable habit. Take any three signals at all — call the first whistle, the second hum, and the third chirp.

He feeds hum through hum and sets the result aside as Relay 1; then he feeds Relay 1 through hum and calls the result Relay 2; then he feeds Relay 2 through chirp and calls that Relay 3; then he feeds Relay 3 through hum and sets the result aside as Relay 4.

However he chooses his three starting signals, whistle itself and Relay 4 always sound exactly alike. That is simply how this station works, without exception.

One night his trainee wonders about something. Take any four signals — again call the first whistle, the second hum, the third chirp, and the fourth buzz. Feed whistle through hum and set the result aside as Relay 1. Separately, feed buzz through hum and call the result Relay 2; then feed buzz through Relay 2 and call that Relay 3; then feed chirp through Relay 3 and set the result aside as Relay 4.

At this station, must Relay 1 and Relay 4 always sound exactly alike?
